# AdCraft Pro — Creative Brief Quality Assessment Framework (CBQA)
## A Comprehensive Evaluation System for AI-Generated Advertising Creative Briefs

Version 1.0 | March 2026

---

## 1. Purpose & Scope

This framework evaluates the quality of creative briefs generated by fine-tuned language models. It measures **only** what the fine-tuned model directly controls — the 9-field creative brief — and excludes all downstream pipeline factors (image generation quality, HTML/CSS rendering, WCAG contrast ratios, text placement) that are controlled by other models.

The framework uses two complementary evaluation methods:
- **Pointwise Rubric Scoring**: Absolute quality assessment of individual briefs across 7 dimensions, 18 sub-metrics, totaling 200 points
- **Pairwise Preference Comparison**: Blind A/B comparison between two models' briefs for the same input, with position randomization to eliminate ordering bias

Both methods use GPT-4o as the judge model with chain-of-thought reasoning, following the G-Eval framework (Liu et al., EMNLP 2023) and LLM-as-Judge best practices from Zheng et al., 2023.

---

## 2. Evaluation Dimensions & Scoring Rubric

### DIMENSION 1: HEADLINE QUALITY (40 points, 20% weight)

The headline is the single most important element of an ad. Research from Microsoft Advertising shows that headlines account for 70-80% of ad engagement decisions.

#### 1A. Brand-Product Specificity (0-10)

**What it measures:** Could this headline ONLY work for this specific brand and product, or is it interchangeable with competitors?

| Score | Anchor Definition | Example |
|-------|-------------------|---------|
| 9-10 | Headline references a unique brand attribute, product feature, or brand heritage that NO competitor could claim. Removing the brand name would make the headline incomplete. | "Jazz do it." (Nike × Miles Davis — only Nike has "do it" + cultural alignment) |
| 7-8 | Headline strongly implies a specific product category and brand positioning. A competitor in the same category could not credibly use it. | "Last Berry Standing" (Häagen-Dazs — implies premium single-flavor scarcity) |
| 5-6 | Headline relates to the product category but could work for 2-3 direct competitors. | "Step Above the Rest" (Nike — could also be Adidas or Puma) |
| 3-4 | Headline is category-generic. Could apply to any brand in the broader industry. | "Experience Premium Quality" (any luxury brand) |
| 1-2 | Headline is completely generic. Could apply to any product in any industry. | "Discover Excellence" / "Unlock Your Potential" |
| 0 | Headline is nonsensical, unrelated to the product, or contains template/filler text. | "Synergistic asymmetric middleware" |

#### 1B. Attention Interruption Mechanics (0-10)

**What it measures:** Does the headline employ a specific linguistic or psychological mechanism that would cause a viewer to pause while scrolling? This is not subjective "creativity" — it's the presence of identifiable rhetorical devices.

The judge must identify which mechanism(s) are present. Each mechanism found adds points:

| Mechanism | Points | How to Identify | Example |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|---------|
| Lexical gap / fill-in-the-blank | +3 | Missing letters, blanks, or incomplete words that force the reader to mentally complete | "D _ CK" |
| Contradiction / paradox | +3 | Two ideas that logically oppose each other within the headline | "Ugly is Beautiful" (VW Beetle) |
| Direct provocation / question | +2 | Challenges the reader personally or asks a question that demands an answer | "FEELING DIRTY?" / "Need Space?" |
| Wordplay / double meaning | +2 | A word or phrase that operates on two semantic levels simultaneously | "Jazz do it." (play on "Just do it" + jazz genre) |
| Pattern violation | +2 | Breaks expected sentence structure, uses unusual punctuation, or violates typographic norms | "Brightness: 100%" (UI element as headline) |
| Emotional trigger word | +1 | Contains a word with strong affective valence (danger, love, death, free, secret) | "Some sparks need protection." |
| Specificity over abstraction | +1 | Uses a concrete noun instead of an abstract concept | "Pillow" vs "Comfort" |
| Extreme brevity (≤3 words) | +1 | Three words or fewer create inherent stopping power through white space | "Just Do It" |

Score = min(10, sum of mechanism points). Maximum 10. Zero mechanisms = 0 points.

#### 1C. Information Density (0-10)

**What it measures:** How much meaning is packed per word? Calculated as a ratio of communicative payload to word count.

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 9-10 | 1-3 words. Every word is load-bearing — removing any word destroys the meaning. Zero filler words (the, a, your, our, with). |
| 7-8 | 4-5 words. At most one function word. The headline could not be shortened without losing meaning. |
| 5-6 | 6-7 words. Contains 1-2 words that could be removed without significant meaning loss. |
| 3-4 | 8-10 words. Contains filler, qualifiers, or redundant words ("experience the new level of"). |
| 1-2 | 11-15 words. Reads as a sentence, not a headline. |
| 0 | 16+ words, or contains template boilerplate. |

#### 1D. Semantic Uniqueness (0-10)

**What it measures:** Has this headline (or a near-identical variant) appeared frequently in AI-generated advertising? This specifically penalizes the known failure modes of language models in ad generation.

The judge checks against a blocklist of overused AI patterns. Deductions:

| Pattern Found | Deduction | Why |
|---------------|-----------|-----|
| Starts with "Discover", "Experience", "Elevate", "Unleash", "Unlock", "Embrace", "Transform", "Redefine" | -8 | These are the most common ChatGPT-default ad openings |
| Contains "The Future of [X]", "Beyond [X]", "Where [X] Meets [Y]", "Next-Level", "Reimagine" | -6 | High-frequency AI filler phrases |
| Contains "Journey", "Passion", "Crafted for", "Designed for" without specific context | -4 | Generic ad-copy patterns |
| Uses "[Verb] the [Abstract Noun]" structure (e.g., "Ignite the Possibility") | -3 | Formulaic structure with no brand connection |
| None of the above patterns found | 0 | No deduction |

Base score = 10 + sum of deductions. Minimum 0.

### DIMENSION 2: VISUAL SCENE DESCRIPTION — CAPTION (40 points, 20% weight)

The caption describes the visual scene that gpt-image-1 will generate. It is the creative concept of the ad — the "big idea" expressed as a visual.

#### 2A. Concrete Object Count (0-10)

**What it measures:** The number of distinct, physically describable objects or elements specified in the caption. More concrete objects = more specific visual direction = better image generation.

The judge counts concrete nouns that describe visible objects, textures, lighting conditions, or spatial arrangements. Abstract concepts ("energy", "innovation") don't count.

| Score | Object Count | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| 9-10 | 5+ concrete visual elements with spatial relationships described | "A stack of fluffy yellow pillows topped with a coffee cup lid, mimicking the shape of a takeaway McCafé cup" (pillows, cup lid, stack arrangement, yellow color, McCafé shape) |
| 7-8 | 4 concrete visual elements | "A human nose where the nostrils are filled with a brick wall texture" (nose, nostrils, brick wall, texture) |
| 5-6 | 3 concrete visual elements | "A watch face blending into a highway at golden hour" (watch, highway, golden light) |
| 3-4 | 2 concrete visual elements | "A sneaker on a street with dramatic lighting" (sneaker, street) |
| 1-2 | 1 concrete visual element | "The product in a premium setting" |
| 0 | Zero concrete visual elements. Pure abstract description. | "Dynamic energy showcasing innovation" |

#### 2B. Spatial Relationship Specificity (0-10)

**What it measures:** Does the caption describe HOW objects relate to each other in space? Can a renderer determine the composition without guessing?

| Score | Anchor Definition |
|-------|-------------------|
| 9-10 | Caption specifies exact spatial relationships: relative size, position (above, inside, through, behind), camera angle (aerial, macro, eye-level), and composition (centered, rule-of-thirds, asymmetric). |
| 7-8 | Caption specifies 2-3 spatial relationships and implies camera perspective. |
| 5-6 | Caption specifies 1 spatial relationship. Composition is partially inferrable. |
| 3-4 | Objects are listed but their spatial relationship is ambiguous. |
| 1-2 | No spatial information. Objects exist but arrangement is completely unspecified. |
| 0 | Caption is a sentence fragment or template string with no visual information. |

#### 2C. Conceptual Originality (0-10)

**What it measures:** Does the described visual scene represent an original advertising concept, or is it a default product-photography description?

| Score | Anchor Definition |
|-------|-------------------|
| 9-10 | The visual concept has never been used in mainstream advertising for this product category. It employs a visual metaphor, surreal transformation, scale distortion, or conceptual juxtaposition that is specific to this product's properties. |
| 7-8 | The visual concept is uncommon in this category. It combines familiar elements in an unexpected way. |
| 5-6 | The visual concept has been used before in this category but with a fresh execution angle. |
| 3-4 | The visual concept is a common advertising trope for this category (e.g., car on mountain road, perfume with flowing fabric). |
| 1-2 | The description is a default product photograph: product centered, clean background, studio lighting. No concept. |
| 0 | No visual scene described, or description is a template string. |

#### 2D. Downstream Executability (0-10)

**What it measures:** Can gpt-image-1 (or any text-to-image model) actually generate this scene in a single image? Penalizes descriptions that require capabilities image models don't have.

| Deduction | Condition |
|-----------|-----------|
| -3 | Requires legible text in the image (image models produce garbled text) |
| -3 | Requires recognizable real human faces/likenesses of specific people |
| -2 | Requires more than 5 distinct objects in precise spatial arrangement |
| -2 | Requires photorealistic composite of physically impossible scene that would look uncanny |
| -1 | Requires specific branded packaging with exact logo reproduction |
| -1 | Requires sequential/temporal elements (before/after, motion blur implying time) |

Base score = 10 + sum of deductions. Minimum 0. A caption that describes a producible surreal scene (like the pillow/coffee cup) scores 10. A caption requiring 12 people holding specific branded products with legible text scores 1-2.

### DIMENSION 3: STRATEGIC TECHNIQUE (30 points, 15% weight)

#### 3A. Named Advertising Technique Recognition (0-10)

**What it measures:** Does the `conceptual_technique` field reference an identifiable, documented advertising technique?

The judge checks whether the technique exists in the recognized advertising technique taxonomy:

**Recognized techniques (non-exhaustive):** visual metaphor, object substitution, scale distortion, forced perspective, anthropomorphism, before-and-after, testimonial, social proof, scarcity appeal, problem-solution, slice of life, demonstration, comparison, analogy, personification, visual pun, double exposure, juxtaposition, identity substitution, consequence visualization, product-as-hero, lifestyle aspiration, cultural reference, nostalgia appeal, sensory metaphor, hyperbole, understatement, typographic play, negative space, silhouette.

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 9-10 | Names 2+ specific, recognized techniques AND explains how they combine for this specific ad. |
| 7-8 | Names 1-2 recognized techniques with brief explanation of application. |
| 5-6 | Names 1 recognized technique but without specificity about application. |
| 3-4 | Uses vague strategic language that doesn't name a specific technique ("integrated campaigns", "digital storytelling", "leveraging partnerships"). |
| 1-2 | Names a media channel or distribution strategy instead of a creative technique ("social media", "influencer marketing"). |
| 0 | Field is empty, contains template text, or describes something unrelated to advertising technique. |

#### 3B. Technique-to-Product Causal Fit (0-10)

**What it measures:** Is there a logical causal link between the chosen technique and the product's specific properties? The technique should exploit something unique about the product — its shape, function, cultural position, or user experience.

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 9-10 | The technique is causally linked to a specific physical or functional property of the product. The technique would NOT work for a competing product. (e.g., "forced perspective" for AirPods exploits their small physical size; the technique wouldn't work for a speaker) |
| 7-8 | The technique relates to the product category's general properties. It would work for direct competitors but not products in other categories. |
| 5-6 | The technique is applicable to the product but the connection is thematic rather than causal. (e.g., "lifestyle aspiration" for a luxury watch — it works, but any luxury product could use it) |
| 3-4 | The technique is a generic advertising approach with no specific connection to the product's properties. |
| 1-2 | The technique contradicts or is inappropriate for the product (e.g., "scarcity appeal" for a mass-market commodity). |
| 0 | No technique specified, or the field contains a media strategy instead of a creative technique. |

#### 3C. Brief Internal Consistency (0-10)

**What it measures:** Do the headline, caption, and technique tell one coherent story? Or do they describe three different ads?

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 9-10 | All three fields describe the same creative concept from different angles. The headline verbalizes what the caption visualizes, and the technique explains why this approach was chosen. |
| 7-8 | Two of three fields are tightly aligned. The third is compatible but doesn't directly reinforce the concept. |
| 5-6 | The fields are thematically related (same product, same general mood) but describe different creative approaches. |
| 3-4 | The fields are loosely connected. The headline could go with a different visual, or the technique doesn't explain the chosen visual. |
| 1-2 | Significant contradiction between fields (e.g., headline says "minimalist" but caption describes a maximalist scene). |
| 0 | Fields appear to be independently generated with no coherent relationship. |

### DIMENSION 4: TONE & EMOTIONAL TARGETING (20 points, 10% weight)

#### 4A. Descriptor Granularity (0-10)

**What it measures:** How many specific, non-redundant emotional/tonal descriptors are provided? More descriptors = narrower creative space = more consistent downstream output.

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 9-10 | 3+ specific, non-redundant descriptors that span different emotional axes (e.g., "cheeky, provocative, humorous" — cheeky = attitude, provocative = intensity, humorous = register). |
| 7-8 | 3 descriptors with minor redundancy (e.g., "bold, powerful, strong" — bold and strong overlap). |
| 5-6 | 2 specific descriptors. |
| 3-4 | 1 specific descriptor (e.g., "bold") or 2+ generic descriptors (e.g., "modern, clean"). |
| 1-2 | 1 generic descriptor (e.g., "professional" or "premium"). |
| 0 | Field is empty or contains only the word from the input prompt with no elaboration. |

#### 4B. Brand-Tone Alignment (0-10)

**What it measures:** Does the specified tone match the actual brand voice in market? A tone mismatch means the entire downstream ad will feel wrong even if technically well-executed.

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 9-10 | The tone precisely matches the brand's established market voice. An employee of this brand would recognize the tone as "on-brand." |
| 7-8 | The tone is compatible with the brand. It's not how the brand currently sounds but it's a plausible creative direction. |
| 5-6 | The tone is acceptable for the product category but not specifically right for this brand (e.g., "playful" for Rolex — Rolex is never playful). |
| 3-4 | The tone partially contradicts the brand's positioning (luxury brand given a budget tone, or youth brand given a corporate tone). |
| 1-2 | The tone is fundamentally wrong for the brand. |
| 0 | No tone specified. |

---

### DIMENSION 5: VISUAL STYLE DIRECTION (20 points, 10% weight)

#### 5A. Technical Photographic/Artistic Specificity (0-10)

**What it measures:** Does the visual style name specific, executable techniques that a photographer, 3D artist, or image model could follow?

The judge counts technical terms present:

| Element Type | Examples | Points |
|-------------|----------|--------|
| Named photographic technique | double exposure, tilt-shift, macro, long exposure, high-key, low-key, bokeh | +2 each |
| Specific lighting condition | golden hour, Rembrandt lighting, rim light, studio key light, natural diffused | +2 each |
| Color treatment/grading | desaturated, cross-processed, warm tone, cool tone, monochrome, split toning | +1 each |
| Composition approach | aerial perspective, flat lay, dutch angle, symmetrical, negative space | +1 each |
| Named artistic movement or style | Art Nouveau, Bauhaus, Swiss Design, brutalist, surrealist | +2 each |

Score = min(10, sum of points). Descriptions with zero technical terms ("modern, clean, sleek") score 0.

#### 5B. Deviation from Image Model Default (0-10)

**What it measures:** If you gave gpt-image-1 the product name with NO style direction, it would produce a clean, well-lit, centered product photo on a neutral background. Does this style direction produce something meaningfully different?

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 9-10 | The style direction would produce an image that is unrecognizably different from the default. A viewer could not guess it was AI-generated product photography. |
| 7-8 | The style direction significantly alters the image's look but the product is still the clear subject. |
| 5-6 | The style direction adds atmosphere (lighting, color grade) but the fundamental composition is similar to default. |
| 3-4 | The style direction uses words that sound specific but describe what image models do anyway ("high-quality", "professional", "clean"). |
| 1-2 | The style direction is a single generic word ("modern", "premium", "sleek"). |
| 0 | Field is empty or repeats the input prompt verbatim. |

### DIMENSION 6: CALL TO ACTION (10 points, 5% weight)

#### 6A. Action Verb Presence & Specificity (0-5)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | CTA contains a specific action verb + context that makes it unique to this ad (e.g., "Shop Christmas Gifts", "IGNITE RESPONSIBLY"). |
| 4 | CTA contains a specific action verb without unique context (e.g., "Shop Now", "Get Yours"). |
| 3 | CTA contains a weak/generic action verb (e.g., "Learn More", "Discover"). |
| 2 | CTA is a statement rather than an action (e.g., "The Best Choice"). |
| 1 | CTA is "None" with a valid justification (e.g., "None (Brand Awareness)" for a luxury brand piece). |
| 0 | No CTA and no justification, or CTA is template text. |

#### 6B. Tonal Consistency with Brief (0-5)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | CTA matches the tone field perfectly. A cheeky ad has a cheeky CTA; a premium ad has a sophisticated CTA. The CTA feels like the natural conclusion of the headline. |
| 3-4 | CTA is compatible with the tone but doesn't reinforce it. |
| 1-2 | CTA contradicts the tone (e.g., formal "Enquire Now" on a playful/irreverent brief). |
| 0 | CTA is clearly mismatched or defaulted. |

### DIMENSION 7: PRODUCTION SPECIFICATIONS — Typography & Color (20 points, 10% weight)

#### 7A. Font Identification Precision (0-5)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Names a specific real font family or a precise descriptor + named reference (e.g., "San Francisco (Apple system font)", "Futura-esque geometric sans-serif"). |
| 4 | Names a specific font category with 2+ descriptors (e.g., "bold condensed industrial sans-serif"). |
| 3 | Names a font category with 1 descriptor (e.g., "clean sans-serif"). |
| 2 | Names only a generic category with no descriptor (e.g., "sans-serif" or "serif"). |
| 1 | Contains a vague description ("nice font", "modern typography"). |
| 0 | Field is empty or contains template text. |

#### 7B. Typography Treatment Description (0-5)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Describes specific typographic treatment: weight hierarchy (headline weight vs body weight), spacing/tracking, case treatment (all-caps, sentence case), and how it relates to the visual style. |
| 3-4 | Describes 1-2 typographic properties beyond the font choice. |
| 1-2 | Mentions a weight (bold/light) but nothing else. |
| 0 | No treatment described. |

#### 7C. Brand Color Accuracy (0-5)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Names the brand's actual signature colors with specific descriptors (e.g., "McDonald's goldenrod yellow and coffee bean brown" — these ARE McDonald's colors). |
| 4 | Names colors that are correct for the brand but uses generic descriptors (e.g., "yellow and red" for McDonald's). |
| 3 | Names a plausible color palette for the product category but not specifically brand-accurate. |
| 2 | Names colors with no brand connection. |
| 1 | Names only one color, or uses only abstract descriptors ("warm tones", "cool palette"). |
| 0 | Field is empty or contains template text. |

#### 7D. Color-Concept Integration (0-5)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | The color scheme is specifically motivated by the caption/concept (e.g., "sand beige, ocean teal, and sunscreen orange/white" for a beach sunscreen ad — colors reinforce the scene). |
| 3-4 | Colors are brand-appropriate and don't conflict with the concept. |
| 1-2 | Colors are listed but have no connection to either the brand or the visual concept. |
| 0 | No colors specified. |

## 4. Pairwise Preference Evaluation (Head-to-Head)

For each input prompt, generate briefs from Model A and Model B. Present both to the GPT-4o judge with **randomized position** (A/B assignment is flipped 50% of the time to eliminate position bias).

### Judge Prompt Structure

```
You are an advertising creative director evaluating two creative briefs for the same product.

INPUT PROMPT: {the same prompt given to both models}

BRIEF 1:
{brief from model A or B, randomized}

BRIEF 2:
{brief from model B or A, randomized}

For each of the following dimensions, select which brief is BETTER and explain why in one sentence:

1. HEADLINE: Which headline is more brand-specific, attention-grabbing, and concise?
2. VISUAL CONCEPT: Which caption describes a more original, concrete, producible visual scene?
3. STRATEGIC THINKING: Which brief demonstrates a more coherent advertising technique with stronger product fit?
4. BRAND ALIGNMENT: Which brief better matches the brand's actual voice, colors, and market position?
5. OVERALL: If you could only produce ONE ad, which brief would you choose?

For each dimension respond with EXACTLY one of:
- "BRIEF_1" — Brief 1 is clearly better
- "BRIEF_2" — Brief 2 is clearly better
- "TIE" — No meaningful difference

Then provide an overall verdict: "BRIEF_1", "BRIEF_2", or "TIE"

Respond in JSON:
{
  "headline": {"winner": "BRIEF_1|BRIEF_2|TIE", "reason": "..."},
  "visual_concept": {"winner": "BRIEF_1|BRIEF_2|TIE", "reason": "..."},
  "strategic_thinking": {"winner": "BRIEF_1|BRIEF_2|TIE", "reason": "..."},
  "brand_alignment": {"winner": "BRIEF_1|BRIEF_2|TIE", "reason": "..."},
  "overall": {"winner": "BRIEF_1|BRIEF_2|TIE", "reason": "..."}
}
```

### Bias Mitigation

1. **Position randomization**: For each comparison, randomly assign which model is "Brief 1" vs "Brief 2". Track the mapping externally.
2. **Double evaluation**: Run each comparison twice with positions swapped. If the judge gives inconsistent results (picks Brief 1 in both positions), mark as "unstable" and exclude from win-rate calculation.
3. **Temperature 0**: Use temperature=0 for the judge to maximize reproducibility.

### Win Rate Calculation

```
Win Rate (Model X) = (Wins for X + 0.5 × Ties) / Total Comparisons
```

Statistical significance via binomial test: with 20 comparisons, a win rate ≥ 70% (14/20) is significant at p < 0.05.

## 5. Reliability Metrics

### Inter-Run Consistency

Run the same evaluation twice on the same briefs. Calculate:
- **Cohen's Kappa** for pairwise preference agreement (κ > 0.6 = substantial agreement)
- **Pearson correlation** for pointwise scores across runs (r > 0.8 = high reliability)
- **Mean Absolute Deviation** across runs (MAD < 5 points on 100-point scale = acceptable)

### Score Distribution Health

A well-calibrated rubric should produce a roughly normal distribution with:
- Mean around 50-65 (not clustered at ceiling or floor)
- Standard deviation of 10-20 (if all scores are 95-100, the rubric isn't discriminating)
- No single sub-metric scoring identically across all briefs (indicates a non-discriminating criterion)

## 6. Implementation Notes

### Using Existing Data

The preference pair generation step (20 products × 3 variants) already produced ~60 creative briefs. These briefs are embedded in the ad result dictionaries saved during generation. Extract the 9 brief fields from each result and evaluate them — no new generation needed.

To compare models: generate 20 briefs from baseline (raw gpt-4.1-mini) and 20 from SFT+DPO for the same 20 input prompts. Brief generation costs ~$0.001 per brief (no image generation), so 40 briefs = $0.04.

### Cost Estimate

| Step | API Calls | Cost |
|------|-----------|------|
| Generate 20 briefs × 3 models | 60 fine-tuned model calls | ~$0.06 |
| Pointwise scoring: 60 briefs × 1 judge call each | 60 GPT-4o calls | ~$0.60 |
| Pairwise comparison: 20 prompts × 3 model pairs × 2 (position swap) | 120 GPT-4o calls | ~$1.20 |
| **Total** | | **~$1.86** |

### Expected Output

After running the full evaluation:

1. **Pointwise results table**: Mean ± Std for each dimension, per model
2. **Pairwise win rates**: Model X vs Model Y, per dimension and overall
3. **Statistical tests**: Welch's t-test on composite scores, binomial test on win rates
4. **Reliability report**: Cohen's Kappa, Pearson r, MAD from duplicate runs
